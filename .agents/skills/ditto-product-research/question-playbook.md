# Question Design Playbook

Patterns and frameworks for writing questions that elicit actionable
insights from Ditto synthetic personas. Based on 50+ production studies.

---

## The Golden Rules

1. **Open-ended, always.** "What's your reaction to X?" not "Do you like X?"
2. **Scenario-based beats abstract.** "Walk me through the last time you..." not "How do you feel about..."
3. **One question per question.** Don't pack three concepts into one prompt.
4. **Never lead.** "What concerns you?" not "Doesn't the high price concern you?"
5. **Emotional context unlocks depth.** "What frustrates you most?" gets richer answers than "What could be improved?"

---

## The 7-Question Framework

This structure works across domains. Adapt the specifics; keep the
sequence. Each question builds on the previous one.

### Q1: Establish Relevance

Ground the persona in their own experience before asking for opinions.

**Pattern:** "Walk me through [activity] in your life. How often do you do it?
What does a typical [session/week/month] look like?"

**Examples:**
- "Walk me through how you manage your personal finances. What tools do you use? What does a typical month look like?"
- "Tell me about the last time you needed to find a new [product/service]. Where did you start?"

**Why it works:** Forces the persona to contextualise their answers in
real behaviour, not hypothetical preferences.

### Q2: Identify Pain

Surface the emotional core of the problem.

**Pattern:** "What's the most frustrating part of [activity]? What makes
you want to [vivid metaphor]?"

**Examples:**
- "What's the most frustrating part of managing prescriptions for your parents? What makes you want to throw your phone out the window?"
- "When you think about [category], what annoys you most? Be specific."

**Why it works:** Vivid metaphors ("throw your laptop", "pull your hair out")
give personas permission to express genuine frustration rather than
polite complaints.

### Q3: Quantify Impact

Turn qualitative pain into measurable cost.

**Pattern:** "Roughly how much [time/money/effort] do you spend dealing
with [problem] per [week/month]? What else could you be doing with that?"

**Examples:**
- "How many hours per week do you spend on healthcare admin for your parents? What does that cost you in terms of work, family time, or sanity?"
- "If you had to put a number on it, how much does [problem] cost you annually — in money, time, or missed opportunities?"

**Why it works:** Quantification transforms vague dissatisfaction into
concrete business cases. The "what else could you be doing" follow-up
reveals opportunity cost.

### Q4: Current Solutions

Map the competitive landscape through behaviour, not brand awareness.

**Pattern:** "What do you currently use to handle [task]? What works?
What doesn't? What have you cobbled together?"

**Examples:**
- "What tools, apps, or workarounds do you currently use for [task]? Be honest — include the spreadsheets, the sticky notes, the 'I just wing it' approach."
- "How do you currently solve [problem]? What's your actual workflow, not the idealised version?"

**Why it works:** The "be honest" and "actual workflow" framing surfaces
the real competitive landscape — which often includes Excel, email, and
manual processes rather than purpose-built tools.

### Q5: Past Attempts

Understand switching behaviour and why previous solutions failed.

**Pattern:** "Have you ever tried a new [tool/service/approach] specifically
to solve [problem]? What happened? Why did you stick with it or abandon it?"

**Examples:**
- "Have you tried any apps or services to help with [task]? What happened? Why did you keep using it — or stop?"
- "Think of the last time you switched from one [product] to another. What triggered the switch? What nearly stopped you?"

**Why it works:** Past behaviour is the strongest predictor of future
behaviour. Switching stories reveal triggers, barriers, and the real
decision criteria.

### Q6: Magic Wand (THE MOST IMPORTANT QUESTION)

This is consistently the most revealing question across all studies.
Personas give you their unfiltered wish list.

**Pattern:** "If you could wave a magic wand and fix ONE thing about
[domain/task/problem], what would it be? Why that one thing above
everything else?"

**Examples:**
- "If you could change one thing about how [category] works, what would it be?"
- "Imagine the perfect [product/service]. What's the single most important thing it does that nothing else does today?"

**Why it works:** The constraint ("ONE thing") forces prioritisation.
The "why that one thing" follow-up reveals the hierarchy of needs.

**What to watch for:** The magic wand answer often DIVERGES from the
pain identified in Q2. The stated pain and the desired solution aren't
always the same — that gap is where product insight lives.

### Q7: Adoption Barriers

Identify deal breakers before you build.

**Pattern:** "Imagine a [product/service] that solves [problem you've
discussed]. What would make you hesitant to try it, even if it could
save you [time/money]?"

**Examples:**
- "Let's say someone built exactly what you described. What would make you NOT sign up? What's the catch that would kill it for you?"
- "Even if this product worked perfectly, what concerns would you have? What would make you walk away?"

**Why it works:** Asking about barriers AFTER establishing desire (Q6)
reveals genuine objections rather than reflexive resistance. The "even
if it saved you time/money" framing pushes past surface-level concerns.

## Specialised Question Patterns

### Pricing Validation

**Don't ask:** "Would you pay $X for this?"
**Do ask:** "At what monthly price would this feel like a no-brainer?
At what price would you start to hesitate? At what price would you say
'absolutely not, no matter how good it is'?"

**The Van Westendorp variant:**
- "At what price would this feel like such a good deal you'd question the quality?"
- "At what price does this feel like a fair deal?"
- "At what price does this start to feel expensive but you'd still consider it?"
- "At what price is this too expensive to even consider?"

### Positioning Tests

Present 3-4 options and ask for gut reactions:

"I'm going to show you four descriptions of the same product. Tell me
which one grabs you most, and why. Be honest — I want your gut reaction,
not a polished answer:

A) [Positioning option 1]
B) [Positioning option 2]
C) [Positioning option 3]
D) [Positioning option 4]"

**Follow up with:** "What's wrong with the ones you didn't choose?"

### Landing Page / Visual Feedback

When uploading images, screenshots, or websites:

"I'm going to show you a landing page for a new product. Spend a moment
looking at it, then tell me:
- What's the first thing you notice?
- What do you think this product does?
- Would you scroll down or leave? Why?
- What's missing that you'd want to see before making a decision?"

### Competitive Switching

"You're currently using [competitor/current solution]. Imagine a new
alternative comes along. What would it need to offer for you to even
consider switching? What would make switching feel risky or not worth
the hassle?"

### Concept Testing

"Here's a one-sentence description of a new product: '[concept]'.
What's your immediate reaction? What excites you? What concerns you?
What questions do you have?"

## Question Design Anti-Patterns

### Leading Questions
- BAD: "Don't you think the price is too high?"
- GOOD: "What's your reaction to the price?"

### Double-Barrelled Questions
- BAD: "What do you think about the design and pricing?"
- GOOD: Ask about design and pricing as separate questions.

### Binary Questions
- BAD: "Would you use this product?"
- GOOD: "Under what circumstances would you consider using this product?"

### Jargon-Heavy Questions
- BAD: "How would you evaluate the UX of this omnichannel experience?"
- GOOD: "Walk me through what it's like to use this. What works? What's confusing?"

### Hypothetical Without Grounding
- BAD: "If a company made X, would you buy it?"
- GOOD: "Think about the last time you dealt with [problem]. If someone offered you [solution] right then, what would you have done?"

## Adapting by Study Type

### Pain Discovery Study
Heavy on Q1-Q3. You're mapping the landscape, not testing solutions.
Let personas vent. Use emotional language in prompts.

### Product Validation Study
Heavy on Q6-Q7. You have a concept; you're testing whether it fits.
Include the concept description in your questions.

### Pricing Study
Replace Q6 with Van Westendorp pricing questions. Add a question about
comparison anchors: "What do you currently pay for the closest alternative?"

### Competitive Intelligence Study
Heavy on Q4-Q5. Map what people actually use, why they chose it, and
what would make them leave. Include competitor names explicitly.

### Multi-Phase Study
Run Pain Discovery first (Q1-Q7), then design a targeted Deep Dive and
Concept Test based on findings. See @examples/carequarter.md for the
full 3-phase pattern.
